# Case Study: Resetting A Weekly Decision Cadence

## Situation

A leadership team has a weekly review, but it is not functioning as a decision system.

Symptoms:

- too much meeting time is spent reading charts live
- ad-hoc requests keep piling up between reviews
- people leave without clear follow-ups
- analytics is seen as a reporting function, not a decision partner

## Intervention

The reset focuses on four changes:

1. narrow the forum to a small set of decision-driving metrics
2. require asynchronous prep before the meeting
3. structure the live review around variances, drivers, and decisions
4. assign owners and due dates before the meeting closes

## Before And After

| Dimension | Before | After |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Meeting behavior | charts are read live | pre-read happens asynchronously |
| Decision quality | discussion drifts | discussion centers on decisions and tradeoffs |
| Follow-through | actions are vague | owners and due dates are explicit |
| Analytics load | ad-hoc pulls keep growing | recurring questions get absorbed into the cadence |

## Signals Of Change

The same forum starts doing different work:

- fewer reactive data pulls
- faster discussion of what changed and why
- more explicit ownership after each review
- stronger confidence in the weekly operating rhythm

## How To Measure The Shift

If you run this change for real, track:

- pre-read completion rate before the weekly review
- number of explicit decisions made per meeting
- number of actions with an owner and due date by meeting close
- volume of recurring ad-hoc requests between decision forums

## Transferable Lesson

The point of a weekly review is not to display information. It is to create a reliable decision cadence. Once that is the design goal, the analytics system, meeting format, and follow-up model all get sharper.
